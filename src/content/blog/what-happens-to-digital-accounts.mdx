A friend lost his dad last year. Amid everything else — the funeral, the paperwork, the grief — there was a small, maddening problem nobody anticipated: his dad's iCloud photos. Thousands of them. Family vacations. Childhood birthdays. The only copies.

Apple needed a death certificate. That took six weeks. And then they needed proof that his dad had named him as a Legacy Contact — which he hadn't. Not because he didn't care. Because he didn't know.

Most of us have 20, 30, 50+ online accounts. Email. Cloud storage. Social media. Crypto. Subscriptions we forgot about. If you lost access tomorrow — any reason — would anyone know what you had, let alone how to get in?

Some of the biggest platforms have built tools for this. They're scattered, incomplete, and almost nobody knows they exist.

## What Each Platform Offers (and What It Actually Takes to Trigger)

| Platform | Tool | What It Does | Trigger |
|----------|------|-------------|---------|
| **Google** | Inactive Account Manager | Shares selected data with people you choose — or optionally deletes everything | 3–18 months of inactivity. No death certificate needed. |
| **Apple** | Legacy Contact | Lets a named person access your iCloud data (photos, notes, mail, files) | Death certificate required. Takes weeks. |
| **Facebook** | Memorialization / Legacy Contact | Freezes your profile as a memorial, or assigns a caretaker to manage it | Someone reports what happened with proof |
| **Instagram** | Memorialization | Similar to Facebook — freeze or remove | Someone reports with proof |
| **X (Twitter)** | Nothing built-in | Family can request deactivation, but there's no official "I can't log in" tool | Manual request only |
| **LinkedIn** | Nothing built-in | Profile removal can be requested by a family member | Manual request only |

Google's tool is the standout. No death certificate, no legal process — just inactivity. Set it to three months, and if you don't check your email, Gmail, or YouTube for that long, Google hands the keys to whoever you named. It's not perfect, but it's the closest thing to automatic.

Apple's requires a death certificate — slow, bureaucratic, and useless during the first weeks when your family is scrambling. If you're just temporarily unable to log in — say you're in the hospital — Apple won't help at all. Their tool is designed for a worst-case scenario, not a "can't reach my phone" scenario.

Facebook, Instagram, X, LinkedIn? All reactive. Someone has to know to contact them, and they all want proof.

## The Three Gaps Nobody Talks About

These tools are individually useful. Together, they leave three big holes.

### 1. They Don't Talk to Each Other

Google doesn't know about your Apple setup. Facebook doesn't know about your Google setup. There's no central inventory. The people you trust have to discover each one individually — and hope they don't miss anything.

### 2. The Most Valuable Stuff Isn't Covered

Think about what's actually valuable in your digital life:

- **Crypto wallets** — Seed phrases and private keys live nowhere official
- **Domain names and hosting** — If your credit card expires, your website goes dark
- **Creator income** — YouTube, Substack, Patreon accounts that might still be earning
- **Cloud infrastructure** — AWS, Vercel, Supabase instances that need paying
- **Steam library, Kindle books** — Hundreds or thousands of dollars in purchases

None of these have a "what if I can't log in" setting. Not one.

### 3. Nobody Knows What You Set Up

You might do everything right — Google's tool, Apple's Legacy Contact, a password manager with emergency access. But if your family doesn't know these things exist and where to look, none of them help. Google won't proactively email your spouse. Apple won't call your kid. The systems sit there, waiting, silently.

## What a Digital Plan Actually Needs

Three things:

1. **A list** — Every account, platform, and asset you have
2. **Instructions** — What should happen to each one (transfer? delete? memorialize?)
3. **A delivery mechanism** — So the right people get the right information at the right time, and not before

The first two you can do right now with a Google Doc. The third one is the hard part — because it's not just storage. It's timing.

Print the list and it's a physical security risk. Put it in a shared password vault and everyone can see it now. Build your own server and — well, who's maintaining it?

## Start Today (Seriously, This Takes 10 Minutes)

**Right now, before you close this tab:** Open a blank note. Write down every digital platform you use. Email, banking, social, cloud, subscriptions, crypto, domain names. Don't worry about passwords. Just the list. Counting them is sobering.

**This week:** Set up the built-in tools on the platforms that have them. About five minutes each:

- [Google Inactive Account Manager](https://myaccount.google.com/inactive) — Set your timeout, name your contacts, decide what to share
- [Apple Legacy Contact](https://appleid.apple.com/account/manage) — Settings → Apple ID → Sign-In & Security → Legacy Contact
- [Facebook Memorialization Settings](https://www.facebook.com/settings?tab=memorialization) — Pick a legacy contact and decide what happens to your profile

**Whenever you're ready:** Think about the stuff the big platforms don't cover. The crypto. The domains. The creator accounts. Some people use a shared password manager with emergency access. Some set up an encrypted vault.
