Your bank accounts eventually get found. Your social media does not. Your game library does not.

The people who built these platforms didn't design for you to stop logging in. When it happens — for any reason — the gap between "they have it" and "nobody can get in" is usually one thing: a password nobody wrote down.

Six stories, all real. Five people lost everything. One didn't. The difference was a plan.

## A Steam Library Built Over 20 Years

In May 2024, a user on the ResetEra forum — let's call him "D" — reached out to Valve. His father was terminally ill. Could he pass his Steam account down through a will?

Valve's response was short and final:

> "Steam accounts and games are non-transferable. Steam Support cannot provide someone else with access to the account or merge its contents with another account. Your Steam account cannot be transferred via a will."

The story went viral. Thousands of gamers realized at the same moment: the library they'd spent decades and thousands of dollars building? It's locked forever. Officially.

The community converged on the same advice, whispered in every thread: *"Just give them the password. Don't tell Valve."*

## An Instagram Income That Vanished Overnight

In the UK, a 28-year-old content creator was earning about £3,500 a month from brand sponsorships and affiliate links on Instagram — roughly £42,000 a year.

He was in a car accident and never recovered.

His family had a death certificate and a grant of probate. What they didn't have was his password. Instagram's memorialization policy is clear: no one logs in. No one transfers the account. No one accesses the linked payment rails. The income stopped the moment he stopped posting.

The brand contracts — about £8,000 in outstanding commitments — went unfulfilled. Not because the family didn't want to. Because the app wouldn't let them.

## The Influencer Whose "Friend" Held the Keys

In China, a Douyin (TikTok) creator named Luo was building something real — over 1.4 million followers, regular brand collaborations, a genuine business.

He passed away in 2023.

When his family tried to recover his account, they discovered the trap he'd walked into: the account was registered under a friend's real-name verification. Someone else's ID, someone else's phone number. That friend, after Luo's passing, quietly removed all third-party login methods, bound their own phone and identity, and took full control. The family's messages went unanswered.

They're now in litigation. Nobody knows how long it will take.

## A Father, a Daughter, and a Game Called Fantasy Westward Journey

In 2018, a 56-year-old man in Taiyuan lost his daughter unexpectedly. She had been a devoted player of Fantasy Westward Journey (梦幻西游), one of China's longest-running MMORPGs. Over the years, she'd invested roughly ¥50,000 — about $7,000 — into her account. Equipment. Rare skins. Years of daily play.

Her father didn't know the account name. He didn't know the password. All he had was a vague memory that she loved this game.

What followed took months: an inheritance notarization, conversations with NetEase's legal department, help from her friends in the game community who pieced together what she owned. He succeeded — but only because three rare things aligned: legal documentation, corporate goodwill, and inside help from fellow players.

Most families don't have one of those, let alone three.

## The Streamer Whose Skins Were Worth More Than His Car

A young Honor of Kings (王者荣耀) streamer passed away suddenly a few years ago. He'd put over ¥100,000 — about $14,000 — into character skins across multiple Tencent games. His father tried to reclaim the accounts.

The notary office refused.

Honor of Kings accounts are tied to QQ numbers, and QQ's terms of service draw a hard line: accounts are licensed, not owned. Non-transferable. No exception. The skins are still there, sitting on Tencent's servers — functional, paid for, unreachable.

## The One Who Got It Right

A 25-year-old man in Zhejiang province — let's call him Chen — walked into a will registry office and made a legal will for his digital assets. His inventory: multiple game accounts across Fantasy Westward Journey and JX3, rare equipment, game currency. Total value: about ¥50,000.

His designated heir: not a family member, but a fellow gamer — someone who understood what the items were worth and how to handle them.

Is 25 young to plan your digital estate? Yes. Did he understand something most people don't? Also yes.

## The Thing Platforms Won't Say Out Loud

Every gamer and creator already knows this: if someone you trust has your login credentials, they can access your accounts. Period.

The terms of service say "non-transferable." But nobody from Valve is showing up at your family's door to check who's typing the password. The issue in these cases wasn't that a platform *blocked* anyone. It's that nobody could even get to the login screen.

The real protection isn't legal. It's practical. Someone you trust needs to know:
- What accounts exist
- How to get in
- What to do with each one

## Why "Just Write It Down" Has Its Own Problems

A notebook works — until someone finds it too early. A shared password manager works — except now the person can see everything right now, not when they're supposed to. A safety deposit box works — if your family knows which bank, which box, and has the key.

You need three things to align:
1. **Security** — Nobody reads it before it's time
2. **Accessibility** — The right people can get it when the time comes
3. **A trigger** — Something that decides when "the time" actually is

That's what a check-in switch is designed for.
